Combatting Fake News

A site to help you fight fake news.

Background


Who Believes Fake News?


Short answer? Everyone. In a 2012 survey on political rumors, 70% of respondents expressed belief in at least one rumor asked about, and these results were well-distributed across the population, crossing many demographics- including political parties. Yes, that means both Republicans and Democrats sometimes hold crazy beliefs and can fall in the fake news trap.


What Influences Belief?


A lot of research has been done to figure out why people believe misinformation. Not much of that research has been conclusive. One thing research has shown is that once you read something false, it tends to stick in your brain you remember it as true even after it has been corrected. This 'continued influence effect', as it is called, seems to be nearly impossible to correct. So, once you accept false information to be true, chances are you will always remember it is true- even if it is corrected.


What Can We Do?


One of the major things we can do to keep ourselves from falling into the fake news trap is to be vigilant. We need to critically read all of our news sources- and be ready to come across misinformation. Research has shown that the continued influence effect can be combatted effectively more often when a person is warned that the information they are reading could be false, and if they receive alternative, true information to replace the misinformation.


This Site


This site wants to help you learn more about where fake news comes from, and to help warn you if an online news article is coming from a less than trustworthy source. Using a database of known fake news sources from PolitiFact, this site maps where fake news comes from, and offers a fake news checker in which you can submit your article URL, and we'll let you know whether it comes from a known fake news site or not. This way, you can be prepared and read your news critically.
